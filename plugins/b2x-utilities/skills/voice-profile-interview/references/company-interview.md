# Company Interview — Question Bank (max 25)

Use this bank when the subject is a company or brand. The voice captured here is the **brand voice**, shared across authors and content types. Companies do not have personal beliefs, so the questions stay strictly on positioning, audience, and style.

## How to use this bank

- Research first. For companies, the website, Company Profile, and LinkedIn usually pre-fill the majority of these.
- Decide who is answering. Ideally a founder, CMO, or head of brand. If the answerer is a marketing coordinator, flag that answers may need a second review from leadership.
- Ask one question per turn. Pre-fill and confirm rather than asking cold when possible.
- Push back on brand-speak. If the client says "approachable thought leader," ask for a sentence or a competitor comparison.

## Guardrails

- Do not ask about the company's political donations, employee personal views, or unrelated beliefs.
- Do ask how the brand handles controversy (Q16 below) — that is a positioning question, not a personal belief.

## The bank — 25 question themes

Numbered for reference, not for order.

### Audience and context (essential)

1. **Primary audience.** Who is the core reader? Job title, company size, industry, buying role.
2. **Secondary audiences.** Who else reads this content? (Candidates, partners, press, analysts.)
3. **Content types.** Which formats does this voice cover? (Website, blog, LinkedIn, email, case studies, decks, proposals, press.)
4. **Journey stage.** Is most of this content top-of-funnel education, mid-funnel consideration, or bottom-of-funnel conversion? (Voice shifts with stage.)

### Brand personality (essential)

5. **Three adjectives.** Three specific words that describe the brand voice. Push back on generic ones.
6. **Brand archetype (optional).** Do you already use an archetype framework (Hero, Sage, Everyman, Outlaw, Rebel, etc.)? If yes, which?
7. **Closest brand voice.** Which other brand's voice is closest to where you want to be? Why?
8. **Brand voice to differentiate from.** Which competitor or category voice do you actively want to sound unlike?

### Tone calibration (essential)

9. **Formal ↔ conversational.** Where does the brand sit, and does it shift by channel?
10. **Reserved ↔ provocative.** Does the brand take strong public positions, or stay neutral?
11. **Corporate ↔ human.** How much of the founder / leader's personality should come through?
12. **Humor policy.** Is humor on-brand? If yes, what kind, in which channels, and what is off-limits?

### Positioning and voice substance (ask only what research could not answer)

13. **Promise.** In one line, what does the brand promise the reader?
14. **Point of view.** What is the brand's "we think X" — the belief that shows up in how it writes?
15. **Enemies and straw men.** What does the brand stand against? (Practices, category assumptions, lazy competitor behavior.)
16. **How you handle bad news or mistakes.** Apologetic, matter-of-fact, transparent, legal-first?

### Mechanics (ask only what research could not answer)

17. **POV.** "We," "I" (founder voice), or third person / brand name?
18. **Sentence length and rhythm.** Punchy and short, steady and medium, or varied?
19. **Structure.** Heavy on headers and lists, or prose-driven?
20. **Emphasis and formatting.** Bold, italics, emoji, exclamation points — what is in, what is out?

### Vocabulary (ask only what research could not answer)

21. **Brand-owned terms.** Proprietary terms, product names, framework names, taglines that must be used verbatim. Capitalization rules.
22. **Banned vocabulary.** Industry buzzwords you refuse to use. (Common offenders: "leverage," "synergy," "disrupt," "game-changer," "solutions," "best-in-class." Ask which of these are on the list.)
23. **Acronyms and jargon.** Technical depth expected of the reader. Spell out on first use, or assume fluency?

### Hard rules and constraints (essential)

24. **Always / Never.** Three rules the brand always follows and three it never breaks. Include any legal or compliance constraints (regulated industry disclaimers, claim substantiation, comparison rules).

### Calibration (essential)

25. **Show me.** Two links: one piece that sounds exactly like the brand, one piece published under the brand that does not. Use both to calibrate the profile.

## Budget heuristic

If the client has a real website, active LinkedIn, and an existing Company Profile in B2X, you should land around **10–13 questions** (mostly 1, 3, 5, 8, 12, 15, 16, 21, 22, 24, 25).

If the brand is new, pre-launch, or rebranding, expect to hit **20–25** because you are creating signal rather than extracting it.

## What to do with conflicting signals

Companies often write in multiple voices across channels (formal on the website, loose on LinkedIn). If research shows inconsistency:

1. Ask the client which channel represents the *intended* voice.
2. Document the intended voice as primary.
3. Capture the acceptable range for channels that deliberately deviate (e.g., "LinkedIn can be 20% more casual than the website").

Do not try to force a single voice if the brand genuinely uses multiple — just name them and show when each applies.
