# Person Interview — Question Bank (max 25)

Use this bank when the subject is an individual (executive, consultant, founder, thought leader). The voice captured here is their **professional** voice, not their worldview.

## How to use this bank

- Research first. Pre-fill every answer you can from the research summary.
- For each question below, decide: already answered, inferred and needs confirming, or truly missing.
- Only ask what you need. Confirmations count toward the 25 cap.
- Phrase confirmation questions like: "From your LinkedIn activity, it looks like you [observation]. Is that accurate, or would you frame it differently?"
- Ask one question per turn. Wait for the answer. Push back on vague answers with "Give me an example" or "What does that look like in a sentence?"

## Guardrails

- No questions about politics, religion, ideology, personal life philosophy, contrarian social takes, or personal values outside of work.
- This is a voice for B2X to use on their behalf, not a therapy session.
- If the client volunteers a personal belief that flavors their writing (e.g., "I am a religious person and it shows up in my metaphors"), capture that as a style note, not as a belief.

## The bank — 25 question themes

Numbered for reference, not for order. Ask in whatever order makes sense in conversation.

### Audience and context (essential)

1. **Primary audience.** Who are you most often writing for? Job title, seniority, industry.
2. **Content types.** Which formats does this voice need to apply to? (LinkedIn posts, blog articles, email, newsletters, keynote, proposals, internal memos.)
3. **Goal of most pieces.** Are you mostly educating, persuading, building credibility, selling, or entertaining?

### Core personality (essential)

4. **Three adjectives.** Pick three words that describe how you want to come across in writing. (If they give clichés like "professional," push for something more specific.)
5. **The closest public voice.** Whose writing sounds closest to how you want to sound? Why?
6. **The voice you do NOT want.** Is there a style or author you actively want to avoid sounding like? What specifically?

### Tone calibration (essential)

7. **Formal ↔ conversational.** Where do you sit on the spectrum for most pieces? Does it shift by format?
8. **Hedged ↔ direct.** Do you state positions cleanly, or do you qualify first?
9. **Serious ↔ playful.** How often does humor show up? What kind — dry, self-deprecating, observational?
10. **How you handle authority.** When you need to sound like you know what you are talking about, what changes? (Sentence length, certainty, examples.)

### Mechanics (ask only what research could not answer)

11. **Sentence length.** Short and punchy, medium and steady, or variable by topic?
12. **Openings.** Do you prefer to set context first, open with a claim, open with a question, or open with a story?
13. **Closings.** How do pieces end? CTA, summary, provocation, next steps — and does it depend on format?
14. **Active vs passive voice.** Strict preference or contextual?
15. **Lists, headers, bullets.** When are they appropriate, when do they feel lazy?
16. **Emphasis rules.** Bold, italics, underline, ALL CAPS, emoji, exclamation points — what are you OK with, what bothers you?

### Vocabulary (ask only what research could not answer)

17. **Words you love.** Verbs, metaphors, or phrases you reach for. Three to five is enough.
18. **Words you ban.** Words or phrases that make you wince. (Common AI tells: "delve," "leverage," "unpack," "navigate," "tapestry," "in today's fast-paced world." Ask if any of these are on their list.)
19. **Acronyms and jargon.** How much industry jargon is OK? Spell out on first use, or assume the audience knows?
20. **Citations.** How do you prefer to attribute sources in the running text? (Inline "according to X," bracket `[1]`, none.)

### Hard rules (essential)

21. **Always.** Name up to three rules you want Claude to always follow when writing as you.
22. **Never.** Name up to three rules Claude should never break.
23. **Topics off-limits.** Anything you will not weigh in on professionally? (Legal advice, medical, financial, competitor naming, client specifics, etc.)

### Calibration and validation (essential — at least one)

24. **Show me a piece that sounds like you.** Link or paste a piece of writing you would not change a word of. (Use this to validate the profile at the end.)
25. **The litmus test.** In one line, what is the question Claude should ask itself before finalizing a draft "as you"? (e.g., "Would I actually say this out loud to my team?")

## Budget heuristic

If research was strong, you should land around 10–14 questions (mostly 1–3, 4, 6, 7, 9, 17, 18, 21–25).

If research was thin or the subject is new to public writing, expect to hit 20–25 and use the full list.

If you are over 20 questions and still have gaps, stop and flag the gaps in the final profile as `Unresolved — revisit on first use`. A shipped profile with gaps is better than one the client never finishes.
